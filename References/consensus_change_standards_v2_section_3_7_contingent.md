## Draft text

### §3.7 Decisions Not to Propose

The framework evaluates proposed consensus changes. It does not evaluate the converse: developers declining to pursue a consensus change in response to an identified network issue. This is an analytical asymmetry. A governance framework that scores only action incentivizes premature action by failing to credit considered inaction. Decisions not to propose are governance decisions, and the documentation rigor §3.1 requires of proposal problem statements applies — with adjustments for the absence of a defined activation moment — to the documentation of a decision not to act.

A decision not to propose should be evaluated against five criteria:

1. **Problem documentation.** Was the underlying issue documented with the rigor §3.1(A) requires of proposal problem statements?
2. **Alternatives considered.** Were non-consensus-change remedies — relay policy changes, default configuration changes, ecosystem coordination — evaluated and the evaluation recorded?
3. **Cost-benefit analysis.** Was the cost of not-acting estimated, and quantified where possible?
4. **Decision rationale published.** Was the decision explained in a forum accessible to the broader community, or made tacitly by individual developer review decisions?
5. **Re-evaluation trigger.** Does the decision specify conditions under which it should be revisited?

The framework remains weighted toward scrutinizing action more heavily than inaction — consensus changes carry chain-split risk and the burden of proof rests on proponents of change — but a framework with no apparatus for evaluating considered inaction is incomplete.
